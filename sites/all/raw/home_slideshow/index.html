Students want universities to help them learn skills.

 

 

 

Universities let them down.

Programming, writing, data analysis... People with skills can make a good living.

 

Universities promise to help people learn skills that will get them a good job.

 

Students give universities money and time. Lots of both.

Universities often fail students. Poorly designed courses. Ill-prepared faculty. An indifferent system.

 

Grads in debt, without the skills they need.

 

Employers can't fill well-paying jobs.

It doesn't have to be this way

 

We know how to help people learn skills. Existing research shows us how.

 

But universities don't use the research. Professors don't study it. They do research. Publish or perish.

Will universities rewire themselves to keep their promises to students?

 

No. Not soon. Current practices are too entrenched.

 

But there are cracks in the system.

Cracks in the system

Most professors say they like interacting with students.

 

Professors like using their skills. Math professors like doing math.

 

Professors or committees choose textbooks. They can choose good ones, as well as bad.

More cracks in the system

In every field, there are professors who care about student learning. They will put in the effort to learn about learning.

 

It doesn't take a Ph.D. to grade intro programming exercises. There are people around the globe who can do it.

CyberCourse

CyberCourse (Cyco for short) is a philosophy, set of techniques, and open source software.

 

Cyco works through the cracks. It can improve courses now, without waiting for universities to fix themselves.

 

It starts with authors...

Authors

Authors are experts in chemistry, analytics, writing, etc. They want to help students learn, but may not know how.

 

The Cyco community helps authors learn about learning. The basics, like outcome-based courses, deep learning, metacognition, and formative feedback.

Authors

Authors create CyberCourses. They're like online textbooks, but written to higher quality standards than traditional textbooks.

 

"Quality" means students learn skills.

 

We already know how to help students. Less reading, more exercises, more feedback.

Authors

Open source Cyco software helps authors create effective courses.

Course design tools link skills, knowledge, and activities (outcome-driven course design).

Grading tools with "clickable rubrics" permit large-scale human grading (formative feeback).

Pseudostudents model good learning (metacognition).

And much more.

Self-sustaining

One Cyco can serve thousands of students.

 

Authors can sell access to their Cycos. They don't get 15% royalties. They get 100% of the revenue.

 

Authors can do good work, set low prices, and still pay the mortgage.

Courses matching students' needs

Authors can serve small markets with courses that are relevant to students' lives.

 

For example, an algebra course for Spanish-speaking students in south Florida. With local landmarks and local issues. When the Miami Marlins play, game stats are in the course the next day.

Running CyberCourses

Flipped, blended, heavy on the assessment.

 

No lectures. With good content, there's no need.

 

Many exercises, perhaps three times as many as traditional courses.

What happens in class?

Professors work with students one-on-one, or in small groups.

 

Help students do tasks. Write programs, balance equations, analyze data...

 

Explain concepts students don't understand.

 

Many profs prefer this to lecturing.

Exercises

Students do tasks, like analyze a data set.

 

Students submit their work online. They get feedback from a human. Maybe not the professor, but someone with enough expertise.

 

Students get a list of things they did right and wrong. Ideally, they get a chance to improve their work, and submit again.

Timing

Students work through courses at their own pace, or at a pace set by the professor.

 

Students who fall are reminded where they should be.

Summary: four roles

Authors make good Cycos.

 

Students run through Cycos, submitting work online.

 

Graders assess their work, suggesting improvements.

 

Professors help students one-on-one.

To use Cyco, universities don't need to change anything about the way they operate.

 

Semesters, classes, sections, credit hours, registration, schedules, grades... all stay the same.
